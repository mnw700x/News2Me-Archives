Pakistan election: phone service suspended as country goes to polls

Caretaker government says it took decision for security reasons after two bomb blasts targeted election offices

Hannah Ellis-Petersen and Shah Meer Baloch in Islamabad
Thu 8 Feb 2024 07.44 ESTFirst published on Thu 8 Feb 2024 04.31 EST

Phone services were suspended in Pakistan on Thursday as the country voted in a general election marred by allegations of vote rigging and militant attacks.

The caretaker government said phone services, including mobile internet, had been suspended for security reasons.

On Wednesday, two bomb blasts targeted election offices in the restive region of Balochistan. The attacks were claimed by Islamic State.

Pakistans interior ministry said as a result of the deteriorating security situation it needed to take measures to safeguard against threats.

Speaking at a polling station in Islamabad, the caretaker interior minister, Gohar Ejaz, insisted he had no other option. Yesterday we lost 28 people in an attack coordinated through mobile phones, he said. I did not want to do it but terrorists can attack anywhere in Pakistan.

More than 128 million people were registered to vote. Polls opened at 8am (0300 GMT) and closed at 5pm.

More than 650,000 security personnel were deployed across Pakistan on election day, with particularly large numbers in the provinces of Balochistan and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, which have been the worst hit by rising militant attacks.

On Thursday, five police officers died in a bomb blast in the Kulachi area of Dera Ismail Khan district in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and one person was killed when gunmen opened fire on a security forces vehicle in Tank, about 25 miles to the north.

In Balochistan, a soldier from a civilian force was killed and 10 others injured in more than a dozen blasts caused by grenades or improvised explosive devices, officials said, while two children died in a blast outside a womens polling station.

The suspension of mobile services drew condemnation from political parties and candidates contesting the election, who said it meant there would be little accountability for actions taken at polling stations and fuelled concerns the election would be neither free nor fair. Amnesty International decried it as a reckless attack on peoples rights.

Some candidates and voters have alleged that the election result could be rigged to ensure that the three-time former prime minister Nawaz Sharif, the head of the Pakistan Muslim League Nawaz (PML-N) is brought back to power.

Sharif is believed to be the favoured candidate of Pakistans powerful military, who are seen as the countrys power brokers and have been accused of rigging past elections.

Mustafa Nawaz Khokhar, who was running as an independent candidate in Islamabad, said on X: Shutting down mobile networks on polling day is the beginning of election day rigging.

The pre-poll environment was already one of the worst in Pakistans history. Cutting candidates off from their agents and staff on election day is unacceptable.

Pakistan Peoples party, which is run by the powerful Bhutto family dynasty, submitted a letter to the chief justice, urging him to order the internet to be restored, alleging that it had impacted voter turnout as the electorate were unable to access information about their polling stations.

Analysts said there had been similar security threats in elections in 2013 and 2018 but there had been no such suspension of phone services.

Pakistan Tehreek-e-Insaf (PTI), the political party of the former prime minister Imran Khan, also called the suspension of mobile services pre-poll rigging, plain and simple.

Analysts believe that a wide voter turnout would benefit PTI. The party has also set up a WhatsApp number where voters can send any evidence of rigging or irregularities at polling stations.

Khan is widely seen as the most popular political figure in Pakistan but he has been jailed since August and was prevented from running in the election. Last week Khan was given three court sentences that would put him behind bars for more than a decade in cases he alleged were politically motivated.

PTI was subjected to a lengthy crackdown in the run-up to the vote, preventing its candidates from being able to contest freely.

On the eve of the elections, PTI and others called for internet services to remain functioning on polling day. A previous high court order had also ruled that the government must provide uninterrupted internet services on polling day.

Its a cowardly move by those in power to manipulate the outcome of the election by cutting off the means of communication and information flow, the PTI said. This is not democracy; this is tyranny disguised as governance.

The party denied widely circulated misinformation on Wednesday night that PTI was boycotting the election.

In polling stations across Islamabad, the mood was overwhelmingly for PTI and Khan, despite his incarceration.

Feroza Rasheed Qureshi, 73, said she could barely walk but had forced her children to carry her to the polling station so she could vote for PTI. We are tired of Sharifs and Bhuttos, she said. What happened to Imran Khan was very wrong, the army is behind the fake cases against him and crackdown on his party.

Pakistan had 20 million first-time voters in this election, who widely appeared to throw their support behind Khan, even as they expressed doubts that the election would be free or fair.

My whole generation is standing with Imran Khan and supporting him, said Areesha Khan, 19. Maybe the result is already decided but we still have be to optimistic that our vote will count and things will change.
